# Industry Adaptation and Manual Scope

Use this reference before design direction, VI expansion, and HTML manual creation.

## Industry Adaptation Rule

A brand identity must look native to its category. Do not default to generic minimal tech, generic luxury, or generic startup aesthetics.

For each project, define:

- Category codes: what visual signals buyers expect in the industry
- Category cliches: what to avoid
- Purchase context: shelf, app, showroom, website, social, signage, packaging
- Trust signals: what makes the brand credible
- Differentiation signals: what makes it memorable
- Production constraints: print, embroidery, hardware engraving, app icon, legal text, packaging substrate

## Category Prompts

### Drinkware / Everyday Product

Use when the brand sells water bottles, tumblers, cups, thermal flasks, coffee cups, or lifestyle containers.

Must consider:

- product body logo placement
- lid engraving or embossed icon
- packaging box and retail label
- ecommerce hero and thumbnail
- material closeups such as powder coating, brushed steel, silicone, glass, or ceramic
- instruction/warranty and care cards
- social avatar and lifestyle post templates
- color/SKU system by size, material, or use scenario

Good visual signals:

- strong silhouette at small sizes
- simple marks that survive cup curvature
- restrained product photography
- tactile material palette
- clear functional information hierarchy

Avoid:

- beach resort aesthetics unless the brand is explicitly travel/leisure
- generic droplet marks
- cheap marketplace badges
- overcomplicated wave illustrations
- illegible marks on curved surfaces
- random business cards as primary applications

### Consumer Technology / Home Appliance

Use when the brand sells vacuums, cleaning devices, robotics, smart home devices, tools, or electronics.

Must consider:

- product body mark and hardware engraving
- app icon and UI compatibility
- packaging and instruction manual clarity
- spec labels and model hierarchy
- retail/e-commerce thumbnail recognition
- engineering credibility without coldness

Good visual signals:

- precision geometry
- airflow/path/sensor logic
- clean negative space
- refined industrial palette
- disciplined typography

Avoid:

- generic SaaS symbols
- cartoon product icons
- cheap appliance badges
- excessive neon/cyberpunk
- fake futuristic interfaces
- overcomplicated mechanical detail

### Food / Beverage

Must consider:

- appetite appeal
- shelf recognition
- packaging front/back hierarchy
- ingredient credibility
- SKU/flavor systems
- regional or craft cues
- print and label constraints

Avoid:

- decorative folk patterns without strategy
- generic organic leaves
- tourist souvenir look
- illegible hand lettering
- fake vintage distress unless brand strategy requires it

### Fashion / Beauty

Must consider:

- packaging finish
- social/editorial art direction
- tactile print details
- wordmark craft
- photography and model rules

Avoid:

- generic luxury spacing without concept
- unreadable monograms
- trend-only aesthetics

### B2B / SaaS / Data

Must consider:

- dashboard/UI usage
- design tokens
- product diagrams
- sales deck and website
- accessibility

Avoid:

- meaningless gradient symbols
- overused node/network marks
- vague innovation language

### Culture / Art / Venue

Must consider:

- poster system
- event typography
- signage
- editorial layouts
- motion/social templates

Avoid:

- sterile corporate look
- generic museum minimalism without attitude

## VI Manual Completeness Levels

### Level 1: Logo Pack

Use only when user explicitly asks for logo only.

Includes:

- approved logo image assets
- icon/wordmark-style image variants
- color variants
- basic clear space and minimum size

### Level 2: Core VI

Default for most small brand requests.

Includes:

- brand foundation summary
- logo system
- color system
- typography
- basic graphic devices
- layout principles
- 3-5 applications
- usage rules

### Level 3: Professional Brand Manual

Use when user asks for VI manual, brand book, full identity, or open-source showcase example.

Includes:

- full brand foundation
- strategy and positioning
- logo system and construction
- misuse rules
- color and typography systems
- imagery/art direction
- iconography/graphic devices
- layout templates
- industry-specific applications
- voice/tone
- design tokens and asset delivery
- HTML manual

### Level 4: Production System

Use when user needs real implementation or launch-ready assets.

Includes Level 3 plus:

- export folders
- print specs
- digital tokens
- component templates
- SKU/template files
- application image generation
- QA checklist

## Application Selection Rules

Choose applications from the brand's real use cases.

Consumer technology examples:

- product body logo placement
- app icon and splash screen
- packaging front/side/back
- instruction manual cover
- warranty card
- e-commerce hero
- comparison/spec card
- retail display panel
- social launch post

Drinkware examples:

- cup body print
- lid engraving
- packaging box
- retail hang tag
- instruction/care card
- ecommerce hero image
- product detail/material closeup
- SKU color lineup
- social avatar and post system
- retail shelf/display strip

Food examples:

- package front/back
- SKU color system
- ingredient photo direction
- shipping box
- gift box
- shelf strip
- e-commerce hero
- social post

Do not show random business cards unless they are relevant.

## Quality Bar

Professional designer-level output should show:

- strong concept before styling
- restrained form
- clear hierarchy
- category-specific touchpoints
- scalable image-based logo logic
- consistent typography and spacing
- realistic production constraints
- no unexplained decoration
